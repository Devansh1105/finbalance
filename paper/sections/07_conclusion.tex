\section{Conclusion}

\benchmark{} turns balance-sheet construction into a controlled
structured-generation benchmark with exact ground truth.
The experiments show that modern language models differ sharply not
only in final score, but also in \emph{how} they fail.
Some models miss the right accounts, some preserve structure but break
the accounting equation, and some nearly solve the task while remaining
weak on retained-earnings closure.

Two conclusions stand out.
First, prompt sensitivity is strongly model-specific:
\CoT{} is transformative for DeepSeek-v3.2 and Qwen~3.5 Flash, but much
smaller and more difficulty-conditional for Gemini~3 Flash.
Second, account-level and factor-level analysis is necessary to
interpret leaderboard scores.
Without it, one would miss both the structural-versus-numerical split
seen in DeepSeek-v3.2 \zs{} and the way retained-earnings closure,
factor sensitivity, and hard-case robustness separate stable systems
from brittle ones.
\benchmark{} also now makes a methodological point:
when model coverage is incomplete, it is better to separate a completed
large-sample study from smaller compatibility runs than to blend them
into a single misleading ranking.
\benchmark{} is therefore most useful not as another finance QA suite,
but as an executable testbed for stateful, constraint-governed
generation in a high-stakes domain.
