\section{Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Helps Through Different Mechanisms}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F3_cot_effect.pdf}
\caption{Effect of chain-of-thought prompting by model.}
\label{fig:cot-effect}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:cot-effect} shows that \CoT{} is not a universal
improvement operator.
DeepSeek-v3.2 benefits the most, followed by Llama~3.3~70B and
gpt-oss-120b.
These gains arise for different reasons in the deeper analysis.
For DeepSeek-v3.2, the gain is primarily arithmetic: mean arithmetic
errors drop by \(3.47\) per problem, and \(87\) of \(100\) problems
improve.
For gpt-oss-120b, the gain is more modest and less stable than for
DeepSeek-v3.2.
For GPT-5.2, \CoT{} is effectively neutral and even reduces
account-level accuracy slightly.

\subsection{Retained Earnings Is the Hardest Account}

\input{tables/account_difficulty}

Table~\ref{tab:account-difficulty} aggregates account-level behavior
over all evaluated systems.
The account difficulty index combines omission and arithmetic error
patterns to identify which accounts are hardest to predict reliably.
Retained Earnings is by far the hardest account, with a \(70.4\%\)
omission rate and only a \(14.0\%\) correct prediction rate.
The next two accounts reveal two different failure modes.
Accumulated Depreciation is often omitted, whereas Owner's Equity is
usually present but numerically wrong.
This distinction matters: the benchmark is not only exposing missing
terminology, but also confusion about how income-statement activity
closes into equity.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F4_account_heatmap.pdf}
\caption{Account omission and arithmetic patterns by model--prompt pair.}
\label{fig:account-heatmap}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:account-heatmap} sharpens this point.
Some models behave as if closing entries do not exist: GPT-5.2, Qwen, and Llama frequently omit Retained Earnings almost entirely.
Other models include the account but miscompute it.
Gemini and DeepSeek under \zs{} are the clearest examples of this second pattern.
gpt-oss-120b is unusual in the opposite direction, reaching the highest \cecr{} values in the benchmark and therefore showing partial closing-entry awareness despite weaker overall robustness.

\subsection{Mixed-Funding Transactions Separate Robust Models from Fragile Ones}

\input{tables/complexity_factors}

Table~\ref{tab:complexity-factors} focuses on the mixed-funding
mechanism because it is the clearest cross-model separator in the
factor-sensitivity analysis.
These transactions require splitting a purchase between cash and debt,
so they test whether the model can maintain multi-account consistency
under partial financing.
GPT-5.2 is nearly invariant to this mechanism, but every weaker family
degrades.
The combination of a large negative \(\Delta\) score and a high
difficulty-curve coefficient of variation is especially revealing for
Llama~3.3~70B and gpt-oss-120b.

\paragraph{Robustness is rare.}
The difficulty-curve analysis shows that GPT-5.2 is the only family
with a robustness coefficient of variation below \(3\%\).
Gemini \CoT{}, Qwen \CoT{}, and DeepSeek \CoT{} fall into a moderate
robustness band with coefficients between \(6.26\%\) and \(7.29\%\).
At the other end, DeepSeek \zs{} and Llama \CoT{} exhibit cliff
behavior: their largest drops occur early and are severe enough to
dominate the aggregate curve.
