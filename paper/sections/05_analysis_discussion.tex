\section{Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Helps Through Different Mechanisms}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F3_cot_effect.pdf}
\caption{Effect of chain-of-thought prompting on the completed
500-problem study.}
\label{fig:cot-effect}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:cot-effect} shows that \CoT{} is not a universal
improvement operator.
DeepSeek-v3.2 benefits the most, followed closely by Qwen~3.5 Flash,
while Gemini~3 Flash benefits only modestly.
These gains arise for different reasons in the deeper analysis.
For DeepSeek-v3.2, the gain is primarily arithmetic:
mean arithmetic errors drop by \(3.78\) per problem, closing-entry
compliance rises by \(27\) percentage points, and \(469\) of the
\(500\) problems improve.
Qwen~3.5 Flash shows a similarly broad gain profile, with \(477\) of
\(500\) problems improving.
Gemini~3 Flash is different: \(275\) problems improve but \(190\)
degrade, and the average benefit appears only once the problems become
hard enough for explicit intermediate reasoning to pay off.

\subsection{Retained Earnings Is the Hardest Account}

\input{tables/account_difficulty}

Table~\ref{tab:account-difficulty} aggregates account-level behavior
over the completed Large-500 runs.
The account difficulty index combines omission and arithmetic error
patterns to identify which accounts are hardest to predict reliably.
Retained Earnings is by far the hardest account, with a \(64.8\%\)
omission rate, a \(26.3\%\) arithmetic-error rate, and only an
\(8.9\%\) correct prediction rate.
The next hardest account, Owner's Equity, reveals a different failure
mode: it is almost never omitted, but it is numerically wrong on
\(65.2\%\) of its occurrences.
This distinction matters.
The benchmark is not only exposing missing terminology, but also
systematic confusion about how income-statement activity closes into
equity.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F4_account_heatmap.pdf}
\caption{Account omission and arithmetic patterns by model--prompt pair
on the completed 500-problem study.}
\label{fig:account-heatmap}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:account-heatmap} sharpens this point.
The strongest \CoT{} runs often behave as if closing entries do not
exist:
Gemini~3 Flash \CoT{} and Qwen~3.5 Flash \CoT{} omit Retained Earnings
on \(94.3\%\) of the problems where it is expected, and Qwen~3.6 Plus
\CoT{} omits it on \(91.3\%\).
The zero-shot runs of DeepSeek-v3.2 and Gemini~3 Flash show the opposite
pattern.
They usually include the account, but miscompute it badly, with
Retained Earnings arithmetic-error rates of \(80.8\%\) and \(77.6\%\),
respectively.
The benchmark therefore distinguishes at least two closing-entry failure
modes: omission and numeric corruption.

\subsection{Factor Sensitivity Still Separates Robust Models from Fragile Ones}

\input{tables/complexity_factors}

Table~\ref{tab:complexity-factors} summarizes each model--prompt pair's
most harmful \emph{controlled} factor, together with the coefficient of
variation of its difficulty curve.
No single mechanism hurts every model in the same way.
Instead, the analysis shows that robustness and factor sensitivity must
be read together.
DeepSeek-v3.2 \CoT{} and Qwen~3.6 Plus \CoT{} are nearly invariant even
to their worst factor, with controlled drops of only \(1.2\) and
\(1.7\) \fbs{} points.
Qwen~3.5 Flash \CoT{}, by contrast, loses \(10.8\) points on
mixed-funding problems after controlling for difficulty, which helps
explain why it trails Qwen~3.6 mainly on the hardest cases.
Among the zero-shot runs, Qwen~3.5 Flash is the most fragile overall:
its worst factor is prepaid adjustments (\(-17.9\) controlled \fbs{}
points), and its difficulty-curve coefficient of variation reaches
\(27.77\%\).

\paragraph{Robustness is rare.}
The difficulty-curve analysis shows that DeepSeek-v3.2 \CoT{} is the
only completed run with both a robustness coefficient of variation below
\(3\%\) and a reverse difficulty curve, ending higher at Level~5 than at
Level~1.
Gemini~3 Flash \CoT{} and Qwen~3.6 Plus \CoT{} form a second tier of
reasonably stable models, while DeepSeek-v3.2 \zs{} and
Qwen~3.5 Flash \zs{} exhibit clear cliff behavior.
Qwen~3.6 Plus also improves over Qwen~3.5 Flash almost entirely through
hard-case robustness, with per-level \CoT{} gains of only \(+0.75\) at
Level~1 but \(+8.84\) at Level~4 and \(+10.03\) at Level~5.
