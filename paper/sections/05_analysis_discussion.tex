\section{Failure Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Helps Through Different Mechanisms}

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F3_cot_effect.pdf}
\caption{Prompt effect by model.}
\label{fig:cot-effect}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:cot-effect} shows that \CoT{} is not a universal improvement operator.
DeepSeek-v3.2 benefits the most, followed by Llama~3.3~70B and gpt-oss-120b.
But these gains arise for different reasons in the deeper error analysis.
For DeepSeek-v3.2, the gain is primarily arithmetic: mean arithmetic errors drop by \(3.47\) per problem, and \(87\) of \(100\) problems improve.
For gpt-oss-120b, the gain is partly superficial: \(17\%\) parse failures in \zs{} shrink to \(4\%\) under \CoT{}, so some of the score increase comes from output recoverability rather than better accounting.
For GPT-5.2, \CoT{} is effectively neutral and even reduces \ala{} slightly.

\subsection{Retained Earnings Is the Hardest Account}

\input{tables/account_difficulty}

Table~\ref{tab:account-difficulty} aggregates account-level behavior over all evaluated systems.
Retained Earnings is by far the hardest account, with a \(70.4\%\) omission rate and only \(14.0\%\) correct prediction rate.
The next two accounts reveal two different failure modes.
Accumulated Depreciation is often omitted, whereas Owner's Equity is usually present but numerically wrong.
This distinction matters: the benchmark is not only exposing missing terminology, but also confusion about how income-statement activity closes into equity.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../figures/F4_account_heatmap.pdf}
\caption{Account omission and arithmetic patterns by model--prompt pair.}
\label{fig:account-heatmap}
\end{figure*}

Figure~\ref{fig:account-heatmap} sharpens this point.
Some models behave as if closing entries do not exist: GPT-5.2, Qwen, and Llama frequently omit Retained Earnings almost entirely.
Other models include the account but miscompute it.
Gemini and DeepSeek under \zs{} are the clearest examples of this second pattern.
gpt-oss-120b is unusual in the opposite direction, reaching the highest \cecr{} values in the benchmark and therefore showing partial closing-entry awareness despite weaker overall robustness.

\subsection{Mixed-Funding Transactions Separate Robust Models from Fragile Ones}

\input{tables/complexity_factors}

Table~\ref{tab:complexity-factors} focuses on the \texttt{mixed\_funding} factor because it is the cleanest cross-model separator in the complexity-attribution analysis.
These transactions require splitting a purchase between cash and debt, so they test whether the model can maintain multi-account consistency under partial financing.
GPT-5.2 is nearly invariant to the factor, but every weaker family degrades.
The combination of a large negative \(\Delta\)\fbs{} and a high difficulty-curve coefficient of variation is especially revealing for Llama~3.3~70B and gpt-oss-120b.

\paragraph{Robustness is rare.}
The difficulty-curve analysis shows that GPT-5.2 is the only family with a robustness CV below \(3\%\).
Gemini \CoT{}, Qwen \CoT{}, and DeepSeek \CoT{} fall into a moderate robustness band with CV between \(6.26\%\) and \(7.29\%\).
At the other end, DeepSeek \zs{} and Llama \CoT{} exhibit cliff behavior: their largest drops occur early and are severe enough to dominate the aggregate curve.
