\begin{abstract}
Existing financial-NLP benchmarks evaluate models on prepared artifacts---filings and pre-extracted tables---measuring comprehension or numerical question answering. Real accounting begins earlier: a packet of source documents must be reconciled into journal entries and aggregated into a balance sheet under double-entry invariants. We introduce \benchmark{}, a multi-document accounting reconciliation benchmark of OCR-style packets across eight industries, three period types, and five difficulty levels, with ground truth produced by a deterministic double-entry ledger and 23 inconsistency codes. We define two metrics: \bsexact{}, the model's self-reported balance sheet, and \bsrecon{}, the balance sheet replayed from the model's own entries through our ledger. Across six frontier LLMs, even the strongest model reaches only 51\% \bsexact{}, and four of six show a 25--38\,pp \aggGap{} between \bsrecon{} and \bsexact{}: entries are largely reconcilable, but the model mis-aggregates its own balance sheet. 52\% of journal entries with correct account and amount cite the wrong supporting documents, and the rate barely moves under explicit citation-pressure prompting. Because accounting admits a closed-form specification, a rule-based \verifier{} returning ledger deltas as one-shot feedback closes the gap by +25--36\,pp across four model families, with an inconsistency-detection trade-off on smaller models. A 75-record validation packet reviewed by practicing finance professionals at two major U.S. investment banks supports benchmark plausibility.
\end{abstract}
