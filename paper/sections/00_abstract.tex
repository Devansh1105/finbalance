\begin{abstract}
  Large language models are increasingly used in finance for document
  analysis, workflow automation, and decision support, but their
  reliability on core accounting tasks remains poorly understood.
  We introduce \benchmark{}, a dataset of 2{,}500 synthetic yet realistic
  accounting problems for evaluating whether a model can construct a
  balance sheet from raw journal entries.
  Each instance is generated by a deterministic ledger engine and paired
  with exact ground truth, which enables precise measurement of equation
  satisfaction, account-level accuracy, account coverage, and
  closing-entry behavior.
  We report two evaluation tiers: a completed stratified 500-problem
  study for the models available in the current OpenRouter/PydanticAI
  pipeline, and a separate paired 100-problem compatibility study for
  frontier models not yet rerun at that scale.
  On the completed 500-problem study, DeepSeek-v3.2 with \CoT{} reaches a
  FinBalance Score of \(92.25\) and \(99.4\%\) balance accuracy, while
  DeepSeek-v3.2 under zero-shot prompting recovers \(96.6\%\) of expected
  accounts yet satisfies the accounting equation only \(11.6\%\) of the
  time.
  The central finding is therefore not merely a ranking difference, but a
  capability split: models often recover the expected account set while
  still violating the accounting equation or mishandling retained-earnings
  closure.
  These results show that fluent financial language does not imply
  reliable bookkeeping, that structural recall and numerical consistency
  come apart, and that exact accounting invariants expose failures that
  are easy to miss in answer-only financial benchmarks.
\end{abstract}
