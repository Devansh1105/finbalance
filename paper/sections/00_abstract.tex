\begin{abstract}
Existing financial-NLP benchmarks evaluate models on prepared artifacts---filings and pre-extracted tables---measuring comprehension or numerical question answering. Real accounting begins earlier: a bundle of source documents must be reconciled into journal entries and aggregated into a balance sheet under double-entry invariants. We introduce \benchmark{}, a multi-document accounting reconciliation benchmark built from source-document bundles with OCR text across eight industries, three period types, and five difficulty levels, with ground truth produced by a deterministic double-entry ledger and 23 inconsistency codes. We define two metrics: \bsexact{}, the model's self-reported balance sheet, and \bsrecon{}, the balance sheet obtained by parsing the model's journal entries, applying them with our deterministic ledger, and checking the resulting balances against ground truth. Across six contemporary LLMs, the highest \bsexact{} score is 51\%, and four of six show a 25--38\,pp \aggGap{} between \bsrecon{} and \bsexact{}: models often recover entries with plausible accounts and amounts, but fail to bind them reliably to supporting documents and to aggregate them into the reported balance sheet. 52\% of journal entries with correct account and amount cite the wrong supporting documents, and the rate barely moves under explicit citation-pressure prompting. A diagnostic ledger-feedback ablation improves reported balance-sheet accuracy by +25--36\,pp across four model families, while exposing an inconsistency-detection trade-off on smaller models. Expert review by practising finance professionals validates the benchmark's accounting analogy and label quality.
\end{abstract}
