\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used in finance for document
analysis, workflow automation, and decision support, but their
reliability on core accounting tasks remains poorly understood.
We introduce \benchmark{}, a dataset of 2{,}500 synthetic yet realistic
accounting problems for evaluating whether a model can construct a
balance sheet from raw journal entries.
Each instance is generated by a deterministic ledger engine and paired
with exact ground truth, which enables precise measurement of equation
satisfaction, account-level accuracy, account coverage, and
closing-entry behavior.
The current manuscript reports a controlled 100-problem cross-model
study with 20 problems at each of five difficulty levels, while also
motivating larger 500-problem evaluations for statistically stronger
open-weight comparisons.
In the controlled study, GPT-5.2 is the strongest overall system,
whereas DeepSeek-v3.2 under zero-shot prompting exhibits a pronounced
structural--numerical split: it recovers most expected accounts yet
satisfies the accounting equation only 15\% of the time.
These results show that fluent financial language does not imply
reliable bookkeeping and motivate both larger-sample evaluation and
tool-augmented agentic variants in which arithmetic is executed outside
the language model.
\end{abstract}
