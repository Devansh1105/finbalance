\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\benchmark{} is difficult for frontier LLMs: no model in our six-LLM panel exceeds 51\% \bsexact{}, and the two strongest---GPT-5-low (51\%) and Grok-4.3 (32\%)---do so for qualitatively different reasons (\S\ref{sec:analysis}). The remaining four cluster between $0.025$ and $0.250$. Figure~\ref{fig:aggregation-gap} decomposes accuracy by plotting \bsexact{} against \bsrecon{}, the balance sheet obtained by deterministically replaying the model's own entries. In four of six models the two metrics disagree dramatically: Gemini~3 Flash writes entries whose replay yields $0.625$ \bsrecon{} but reports a balance sheet matching ground truth only $0.250$ of the time, an \aggGap{} of $37.5$\,pp. DeepSeek V3.2 ($+29.2$\,pp), Claude Haiku~4.5 ($+28.3$\,pp), and Qwen~3 235B ($+25.0$\,pp) show the same pattern. The model has the components of a correct answer in its own intermediate output and fails to aggregate them into a self-consistent statement.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.88\textwidth]{figures/fig_aggregation_gap.pdf}
\caption{\bsrecon{} versus \bsexact{} on the coverage split. Four of six models show a 25--38\,pp aggregation gap; GPT-5-low and Grok-4.3 do not.}
\label{fig:aggregation-gap}
\end{figure*}

If the gap is a self-consistency failure rather than an entries deficit, then giving the model a closed-form view of its own inconsistency should close it. We test this with the \verifier{} of \S\ref{sec:verifier}: the same ledger that produces our ground truth compares the model's reported balance sheet against the one its entries imply, returning per-section deltas as one-shot feedback. The model is given one turn to revise. Figure~\ref{fig:verifier-effects} reports the effect across four gap-having model families. \bsexact{} gains $+25$ to $+37$\,pp, all significant at $95\%$ paired bootstrap. The intervention requires no fine-tuning, no separate verifier model, and feeds no ground-truth values or entry-level hints---only the model's own implied-versus-reported deltas. Recovering this much \bsexact{} with no parametric change supports the self-consistency reading.

The intervention has a real cost, which we report rather than suppress. Figure~\ref{fig:verifier-effects} (right panel, \texttt{inc\_code} column) shows the same verifier's effect on \incCode{} (23 forced-inconsistency packets). Gemini~3 Flash loses $-13$\,pp (not significant), but Claude Haiku~4.5 loses $-52$\,pp and DeepSeek V3.2 loses $-61$\,pp, both significant. When an inconsistency packet's first-pass submission does not raise the flag, the verifier sends ledger deltas instead of giving the model a chance to reconsider whether the documents themselves contradict; the deltas bias the next turn toward reconciling rather than flagging. The trade-off is mild for strong detectors and severe for weak ones.

The aggregation gap is one of two large failure modes; the other is document-linking. On the baseline anchor run, $52\%$ of journal entries with correct account and amount cite the \emph{wrong} supporting documents---which is why the strict joint metric collapses to $5\%$ despite a $57\%$ account-and-amount entry-match rate. A citation-pressure prompt moves the strict mismatch rate from $0.524$ to $0.510$ ($-1.4$\,pp, CI $[-2.9, 0.0]$), borderline at best. Under an \emph{evidence-only} oracle restricting visible documents to ground-truth-cited evidence, the same rate drops by $-26.4$\,pp (CI $[-29.8, -23.0]$). The failure is retrieval-disambiguation, not prompt compliance (Figure~\ref{fig:doc-refs-persistence}).

An alternative to verifier-driven revision is to sample multiple candidates and pick. A best-of-3 condition at temperature $0.7$ with a deterministic ledger-aware selector closes the gap by $+13.3$\,pp \bsexact{} ($n{=}45$ paired); the \verifier{} closes $+32.5$\,pp on the same model. We are explicit that the selector is ledger-aware---this is not pure majority-vote self-consistency---but the comparison is informative: picking recovers $\sim$40\% of the available gain, revising recovers the remainder. The verifier's advantage is not access to the ledger (both conditions have it) but the chance to act on what the ledger says.

Figure~\ref{fig:ablation-deltas} summarises the full ablation matrix on the anchor model. Forced-verifier variants are the only treatments that move \bsexact{} substantially upward; removing scaffolding (allowed accounts, support docs) is the most damaging intervention. Adding $5$, $15$, or $30$ distractor documents decreases \bsrecon{} by $16.7$, $15.8$, and $18.3$\,pp respectively: the penalty saturates after the first few.
