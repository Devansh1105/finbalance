\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\benchmark{} is difficult for frontier LLMs. Figure~\ref{fig:model-accuracy} gives the baseline profile before any intervention: no model in our six-LLM panel exceeds 51\% \bsexact{}, strict document-grounded end-to-end accuracy stays near the floor, and the best entry-level accounting scores are much higher than the final balance-sheet scores. The task is therefore not a simple JSON-formatting problem. Models often identify plausible postings, but they lose correctness when those local decisions must be tied back to documents and aggregated into the final statement.

Table~\ref{tab:aggregation-gap} isolates the central pattern by comparing \bsexact{} against \bsrecon{}, the balance sheet obtained by deterministically replaying the model's own entries. In four of six models the two metrics disagree dramatically: Gemini~3 Flash writes entries whose replay yields $0.625$ \bsrecon{} but reports a balance sheet matching ground truth only $0.250$ of the time, an \aggGap{} of $37.5$\,pp. DeepSeek V3.2 ($+29.2$\,pp), Claude Haiku~4.5 ($+28.3$\,pp), and Qwen~3 235B ($+25.0$\,pp) show the same pattern. The model has the components of a correct answer in its own intermediate output and fails to aggregate them into a self-consistent statement. The two strongest baselines---GPT-5-low (51\% \bsexact{}) and Grok-4.3 (32\%)---lack this gap for different reasons, which we unpack in \S\ref{sec:analysis}.

If the gap is a self-consistency failure rather than an entries deficit, then giving the model a ledger-computed view of its own inconsistency should close it. We test this with the \verifier{} of \S\ref{sec:verifier}: the same ledger that produces our ground truth compares the model's reported balance sheet against the one its entries imply, returning per-section deltas as one-shot feedback. The model is given one turn to revise. Figure~\ref{fig:verifier-effects} reports the effect across four gap-having model families. \bsexact{} gains $+25$ to $+37$\,pp, all significant at $95\%$ paired bootstrap. The intervention requires no fine-tuning, no separate verifier model, and feeds no ground-truth values or entry-level hints---only the model's own implied-versus-reported deltas. Recovering this much \bsexact{} with no parametric change supports the self-consistency reading.

The intervention has a real cost, which we report rather than suppress. Figure~\ref{fig:verifier-effects} (right panel, \texttt{inc\_code} column) shows the same verifier's effect on \incCode{} (23 forced-inconsistency packets). Gemini~3 Flash loses $-13$\,pp (not significant), but Claude Haiku~4.5 loses $-52$\,pp and DeepSeek V3.2 loses $-61$\,pp, both significant. When an inconsistency packet's first-pass submission does not raise the flag, the verifier sends ledger deltas instead of giving the model a chance to reconsider whether the documents themselves contradict; the deltas bias the next turn toward reconciling rather than flagging. The trade-off is mild for strong detectors and severe for weak ones.

The aggregation gap is one of two large failure modes; the other is document-linking. On the baseline anchor run, $52\%$ of journal entries with correct account and amount cite the \emph{wrong} supporting documents---which is why the strict joint metric collapses to $5\%$ despite a $57\%$ account-and-amount entry-match rate. A citation-pressure prompt moves the strict mismatch rate from $0.524$ to $0.510$ ($-1.4$\,pp, CI $[-2.9, 0.0]$), borderline at best. Under an \emph{evidence-only} oracle restricting visible documents to ground-truth-cited evidence, the same rate drops by $-26.4$\,pp (CI $[-29.8, -23.0]$). The failure is retrieval-disambiguation, not prompt compliance (Figure~\ref{fig:doc-refs-persistence}).

An alternative to verifier-driven revision is to sample multiple candidates and pick. We run this only on the 45 standard records where the one-shot Gemini answer has the key gap pattern: replaying its entries gives the correct balance sheet, but its reported balance sheet is wrong. A best-of-3 condition at temperature $0.7$ uses a deterministic ledger-aware selector that prioritizes parse-valid, non-inconsistency candidates whose submitted balance sheet matches ledger replay, then balanced sheets, majority balance-sheet vote, fewer replay deltas, and smaller total delta. On this targeted subset, best-of-3 raises \bsexact{} from $0\%$ to $28.9\%$ (CI $[15.6,42.2]$), while the \verifier{} raises it to $84.4\%$ (CI $[73.3,93.3]$ vs.\ baseline). We are explicit that the selector is ledger-aware---this is not pure majority-vote self-consistency---but the comparison is informative: picking a better draft recovers part of the gap; revising after structured ledger feedback recovers much more.

The context-stress sweep in Figure~\ref{fig:context-stress} separates two concerns that are easy to conflate. Removing irrelevant documents alone is not enough to solve the task, but adding $5$, $15$, or $30$ distractors to an evidence packet decreases \bsrecon{} by $16.7$, $15.8$, and $18.3$\,pp respectively: the penalty appears quickly and then saturates. This supports the interpretation that long context is part of the difficulty, but not the whole explanation; accounting dependencies and document choice remain binding even when context is controlled.
