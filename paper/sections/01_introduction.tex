\section{Introduction}

Large language models are already being deployed across the finance
industry for document understanding, retrieval, drafting, and
decision-support workflows.
Most of that progress, however, has been measured on tasks where the
model reads an existing financial artifact and answers questions about
it.
Accounting work introduces a stricter requirement: the system must
construct a valid artifact whose entries satisfy hard numerical and
structural constraints.

Balance-sheet preparation is a particularly revealing test case.
Given opening balances and a stream of journal entries, a correct
system must identify the affected accounts, maintain running balances,
apply adjusting entries, close temporary accounts into retained
earnings, and verify that assets equal liabilities plus equity.
The task is deterministic for a ledger engine, but it is demanding for
a language model because it couples long-horizon state tracking with a
global conservation law.

This paper studies that setting through \benchmark{}, a benchmark of
2{,}500 synthetic, realistic accounting problems for balance-sheet
construction.
Each problem contains opening balances, journal activity, and
optionally end-of-period adjustments; the model must return a structured
balance sheet with \texttt{assets}, \texttt{liabilities}, and
\texttt{equity}.
Because every instance is generated and scored against a deterministic
ledger engine, the benchmark supports exact evaluation rather than
approximate rubric grading.

Appendix~\ref{app:glossary} supplies a glossary of financial terms for
readers who do not work in accounting.

Figure~\ref{fig:overview} summarizes the benchmark pipeline.
\benchmark{} begins with a deterministic problem generator that
produces 2{,}500 problems across five difficulty levels.
The evaluation layer then prompts each model, recovers the final
structured answer, and scores it with accounting-specific metrics.
A final analysis layer aggregates the results into leaderboard metrics,
account-level error summaries, factor-sensitivity analyses, and
statistical comparisons.

The current paper reports two empirical tiers rather than a single
uniform comparison pool.
The main analysis uses a completed stratified 500-problem subset with
100 problems at each difficulty level.
That larger study covers the model families available in the current
OpenRouter/PydanticAI pipeline and powers the new figures, difficulty
curves, and significance tests.
For frontier systems that have not yet been rerun at that scale, we
retain a separate paired 100-problem compatibility study.
Across both tiers, the benchmark exposes a sharp capability split:
models can recover the right account vocabulary and overall section
structure while still failing the bookkeeping invariants that make the
final statement valid.
DeepSeek-v3.2 under zero-shot prompting on the 500-problem study is the
clearest example, with high account coverage but near-total collapse in
equation satisfaction after the easiest level.

Three research questions organize the paper:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}]
  \item How well do modern language models construct balance sheets as
    accounting mechanisms compound across five difficulty levels, from
    cash-only bookkeeping at Level~1 to dense multi-mechanism problems at
    Level~5?
  \item When prompting changes from direct zero-shot generation to
    reflective chain-of-thought prompting~\citep{wei2022chainofthought},
    which models improve, and do those gains reflect better accounting?
  \item Which accounts and accounting mechanisms contribute most strongly
    to benchmark difficulty, and what do those patterns imply for future
    constructor and auditor systems?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes five contributions:
(1) it introduces a deterministic 2{,}500-problem benchmark for
balance-sheet construction from journal entries across five difficulty
levels;
(2) it defines an executable evaluation protocol in which exact
ground truth is derived from a ledger engine rather than rubric-based
annotation;
(3) it presents an accounting-specific metric suite covering equation
satisfaction, account-level accuracy, account coverage, closing-entry
compliance, and a composite FinBalance Score;
(4) it reports a completed 500-problem evaluation for the models
available in the current OpenRouter/PydanticAI pipeline, alongside a
separate 100-problem compatibility study for frontier models that are
not yet fully rerun at that scale; and
(5) it identifies recurrent failure modes that standard answer-only
finance benchmarks obscure, including arithmetic collapse despite high
structural recall, difficulty-conditional \CoT{} effects, and
closing-entry blindness.
