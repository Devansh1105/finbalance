\section{Introduction}

Large language models are already being deployed across the finance
industry for document understanding, retrieval, drafting, and
decision-support workflows.
Most of that progress, however, has been measured on tasks where the
model reads an existing financial artifact and answers questions about
it, as in benchmarks such as
FinQA~\citep{chen2021finqa},
TAT-QA~\citep{zhu2021tatqa},
DocFinQA~\citep{reddy2024docfinqa}, and
FinanceBench~\citep{islam2023financebench}.
Accounting work introduces a stricter requirement: the system must
construct a valid artifact whose entries satisfy hard numerical and
structural constraints.

Balance-sheet preparation is a particularly revealing test case.
Given opening balances and a stream of journal entries, a correct
system must identify the affected accounts, maintain running balances,
apply adjusting entries, close temporary accounts into retained
earnings, and verify that assets equal liabilities plus equity.
The task is deterministic for a ledger engine, but it is demanding for
a language model because it couples long-horizon state tracking with a
global conservation law.
That distinguishes it from both answer-only financial QA benchmarks and
recent audit-oriented settings that begin from existing statements or
disclosures~\citep{wang2025finauditing,wang2025automating}.

This paper studies that setting through \benchmark{}, a dataset of
2{,}500 synthetic, realistic accounting problems for balance-sheet
construction.
Each problem contains opening balances, journal activity, and
optionally end-of-period adjustments; the model must return a structured
balance sheet with \texttt{assets}, \texttt{liabilities}, and
\texttt{equity}.
Because every instance is generated and scored against a deterministic
ledger engine, the benchmark supports exact evaluation rather than
approximate rubric grading.

Appendix~\ref{app:glossary} supplies a glossary of financial terms for

We evaluate seven model families on a stratified 500-problem study
and report a separate 100-problem compatibility study for additional
frontier models.
Across both tiers, the benchmark exposes a sharp capability split:
models often recover the expected account set while still violating
the accounting equation or mishandling retained-earnings closure.

Three research questions organize the paper:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}]
  \item How well do modern language models construct balance sheets as
    accounting mechanisms compound across five difficulty levels, from
    cash-only bookkeeping at Level~1 to dense multi-mechanism problems at
    Level~5?
  \item When prompting changes from direct zero-shot generation to
    reflective chain-of-thought prompting~\citep{wei2022chainofthought},
    which models improve, and do those gains reflect better accounting?
  \item Which accounts and accounting mechanisms contribute most strongly
    to benchmark difficulty, and what do those patterns imply for future
    constructor and auditor systems?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(1)~A deterministic 2{,}500-problem benchmark for balance-sheet
construction from journal entries across five difficulty levels;
(2)~an executable evaluation protocol with exact ledger-derived ground
truth and an accounting-specific metric suite;
(3)~a 500-problem study of seven model families across \zs{} and \CoT{}
prompting, with paired significance tests on the matched runs; and
(4)~identification of recurrent failure modes---arithmetic collapse
despite high structural recall, difficulty-conditional \CoT{} effects,
and closing-entry blindness---that answer-only finance benchmarks
obscure.
