\section{Introduction}

Preparing a balance sheet from a sequence of journal entries is,
in principle, deterministic: a correct ledger engine can do it
without error.  Yet the same task becomes a genuine reasoning
challenge when posed to a language model because the model
receives only the natural-language transaction descriptions and
must internally reconstruct the double-entry logic---choosing
which accounts to debit and credit, accumulating running
balances, closing revenue and expense into equity, and verifying
that Assets~$=$~Liabilities~$+$~Equity.  No single rule or
formula suffices; the model must chain heterogeneous accounting
steps while maintaining a global conservation constraint across
every account it emits.

\subsection{Related Work}

A growing body of financial NLP benchmarks has emerged, but all
target \emph{comprehension} of pre-existing documents rather than
\emph{construction} of new ones.
FinQA~\citep{chen2021finqa} and
ConvFinQA~\citep{chen2022convfinqa} pose numerical reasoning
questions over corporate earnings reports;
TAT-QA~\citep{zhu2021tatqa} adds hybrid tabular--textual
contexts; and DocFinQA~\citep{reddy2024docfinqa} embeds similar
questions in full-length SEC filings.
Each requires the model to \emph{read} a financial table and
compute an answer---never to \emph{produce} one from scratch.
Broader evaluation suites---FLUE~\citep{shah2022flueflang},
PIXIU~\citep{xie2023pixiu}, FinBen~\citep{xie2024finben}, and
FinanceBench~\citep{islam2023financebench}---aggregate tasks
spanning sentiment analysis, named-entity recognition, stock-movement
prediction, and open-book QA, yet none includes end-to-end
financial statement preparation.
Even MMLU~\citep{hendrycks2021mmlu}, which contains
professional-accounting questions, tests only declarative
knowledge through multiple choice rather than procedural
application.

The closest prior work, \citet{kim2024financial}, feeds
\emph{existing} balance sheets and income statements to GPT-4
and evaluates whether the model can predict earnings
direction---a complementary but fundamentally different task from
generating a statement \emph{de novo}.
Multi-step arithmetic benchmarks such as
GSM8K~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k} stress numerical reasoning but
outside any domain-specific accounting structure.
To our knowledge, no existing benchmark asks a model to construct
a complete, internally consistent balance sheet from raw journal
entries---the gap \benchmark{} is designed to fill.

\subsection{Benchmark Overview}

\benchmark{} targets this generative gap directly.
Each problem starts from opening balances, presents a sequence of
double-entry journal events, optionally adds end-of-period
adjustments, and asks the model to emit a JSON balance sheet with
\texttt{assets}, \texttt{liabilities}, and \texttt{equity}.
The benchmark is built around a deterministic ledger engine, so
every instance has exact ground truth by construction.
This lets us separate surface-level formatting issues from genuine
failures in statement preparation, account classification, equity
closure, and arithmetic consistency.

In the controlled Small-100 study, a stratified 100-problem subset
with 20 problems from each difficulty level, GPT-5.2 stays closest
to the oracle ceiling and reaches \fbs{} $=90.85$ with perfect
\ba{}, whereas most other models deteriorate markedly at
Levels~4--5.
The benchmark also exposes a structural--numerical split:
DeepSeek-v3.2 under \zs{} recalls \(96.8\%\) of expected accounts
yet balances only \(15\%\) of statements.
Retained Earnings emerges as the hardest account overall, and
mixed-funding transactions are a consistent separator between
robust and fragile systems.

Three research questions organize the paper:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt,label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*:}]
\item How well do modern language models prepare and verify balance
  sheets as accounting mechanisms compound across five difficulty
  levels (ranging from cash-only bookkeeping at Level~1 to problems
  combining credit transactions, COGS, mixed funding, and
  derived interest at Level~5; see Table~\ref{tab:dataset-summary})?
\item When supervision increases from \zs{} prompting to reflective
  \CoT{} prompting~\citep{wei2022chainofthought}, which models
  improve, and do those gains reflect better accounting or merely
  more recoverable output structure?
\item Which accounts and transaction factors dominate benchmark
  difficulty once per-problem outputs are retained for secondary
  analysis?
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes five contributions:
(1) a deterministic 2{,}500-problem dataset for balance-sheet construction from journal entries across five difficulty levels;
(2) an automated, flexible benchmarking harness that varies model backends and prompting strategies, recovers structured outputs when possible, and retains per-problem artifacts;
(3) an accounting-specific evaluation suite centered on \fbs{}, \ba{}, \ala{}, \acr{}, and \cecr{};
(4) a controlled six-model Small-100 comparison under matched \zs{} and \CoT{} settings; and
(5) an analysis of recurring failure modes, including arithmetic collapse under high structural recall, closing-entry blindness, and mixed-funding fragility.
