  FINDING 1 — Three-Tier Capability Hierarchy (Statistically Significant)
================================================================================

  Tier 1 — Frontier (FBS ~90):    GPT-5.2   ZS: BA=98%  FBS=90.2  BEM=$0
                                   GPT-5.2  CoT: BA=100% FBS=90.9  BEM=$0

  Tier 2 — Mid-tier (FBS ~79-83): Gemini    ZS: BA=73%  FBS=79.0  BEM=$4,376
                                   Gemini   CoT: BA=80%  FBS=83.5  BEM=$4,630

  Tier 3 — Failing  (FBS ~33):    Llama     ZS: BA=2%   FBS=33.4  BEM=$56,856

  Rule-based Oracle:                          BA=100% FBS=99.97 BEM=$0

  Pairwise Permutation Tests (n=10,000):
    GPT-5.2 vs Gemini:    Delta=+7.4-11.8 pts, p<0.001  ***
    GPT-5.2 vs Llama:     Delta=+56.8-57.4 pts, p<0.001 ***
    Gemini vs Llama:      Delta=+45.6-50.0 pts, p<0.001 ***
    GPT-5.2 CoT vs ZS:    Delta=+0.65 pts,      p=0.533 n.s.
    Gemini CoT vs ZS:     Delta=+4.4 pts,        p=0.062 marginal

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  The benchmark discriminates between three capability tiers with p<0.001
  for every inter-tier gap. This validates FinBalance as a discriminative
  benchmark — a key reviewer concern for any new benchmark paper. The
  non-significant CoT vs zero-shot gap for GPT-5.2 (p=0.53) is itself a
  finding: frontier models do not benefit from extended reasoning on this
  task in terms of FBS, only in terms of BA (parse safety).

  Use this in: Section 4 Main Results, Table 2 (leaderboard), Section 5.1
  statistical validity.


================================================================================
  FINDING 2 — The Closing-Entry Blindness Paradox (Headline Finding)
================================================================================

  GPT-5.2 Retained Earnings compliance (CECR):
    Zero-shot:  8%   (omission rate=92.8%, arithmetic error rate=0%)
    CoT:        1%   (CoT worsens this — omission rate approaches 99%)

  GPT-5.2 CECR by level (zero-shot):  L1=10%, L2=10%, L3=5%, L4=5%, L5=10%
  GPT-5.2 CECR by level (CoT):        L1=5%,  L2=0%,  L3=0%, L4=0%, L5=0%

  Gemini Retained Earnings compliance (CECR):
    Zero-shot: 15%   (omission=10.1%, arithmetic error=75.8%)
    CoT:       17%   (mixed: CECR improves at L1/L3, degrades at L2/L4)

  Llama CECR: 1% across all levels.

  The paradox: GPT-5.2 achieves BA=98-100% (perfect equation balance)
  but CECR=1-8% (almost never correct closing entries). The model satisfies
  Assets = Liabilities + Equity by inflating Owner's Equity rather than
  correctly splitting equity into Owner's Equity + Retained Earnings as
  GAAP requires. The equation is satisfied for the wrong structural reason.

  GPT-5.2 zero-shot error breakdown:
    OE (Omission):      44.5%  -- missing RE drives this entirely
    CO (Hallucination): 34.5%  -- Net Income hallucinated instead of RE
    AE (Arithmetic):    13.5%
    CV (Classification): 6.8%

  GPT-5.2 CoT error breakdown:
    OE: 45.6%  (+1.1pp -- RE omission persists and slightly worsens)
    CO: 28.7%  (-5.8pp -- fewer hallucinations overall)
    AE: 19.3%  (+5.8pp -- CoT introduces more arithmetic mistakes)

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  This is the headline finding. It shows BA alone is insufficient -- a
  model can score BA=100% while being conceptually wrong on the GAAP
  closing-entry step. CECR is the metric that exposes this. The paradox
  also gives the paper a concrete "surprising result": the best model
  scores highest on the equation but lowest on conceptual compliance.

  Quotable for abstract: "GPT-5.2 achieves BA=98-100% yet correctly
  produces Retained Earnings in only 1-8% of responses — satisfying the
  accounting equation by inflating Owner's Equity rather than performing
  the closing-entry step mandated by GAAP."

  Use this in: Section 1 Introduction, Section 4.2 Closing-Entry Analysis,
  Section 5 Discussion.


================================================================================
  FINDING 3 — Two Distinct Failure Modes (ACR + BEM make this visible)
================================================================================

  GPT-5.2 profile:  ACR=86%  BA=98%  BEM=$0      ALA=0.827
  Gemini profile:   ACR=98%  BA=73%  BEM=$4,376  ALA=0.719

  GPT-5.2 failure mode — STRUCTURAL OMISSION:
    - Computes present accounts correctly (BA=98%, AE only 13.5% of errors)
    - But systematically omits accounts (OE=44.5%, driven entirely by RE)
    - Missing accounts are compensated by inflating Owner's Equity
    - ACR=86%: 14% of expected accounts consistently absent from output
    - BEM~$0: omission + compensation keeps the equation exactly balanced

  Gemini failure mode — ARITHMETIC COLLAPSE:
    - Identifies nearly all expected accounts (ACR=98% zero-shot)
    - But cannot compute their values correctly (AE=81.4% of all errors)
    - Cash arithmetic error rate: 86% (wrong value in 86/100 problems)
    - RE arithmetic error rate: 75.8% (present but computed incorrectly)
    - BEM=$4,376: wrong values create real imbalances in the equation
    - BA=73%: wrong numbers prevent the equation from balancing

  This contrast is invisible from BA or ALA alone. You need:
    - ACR to reveal Gemini has the accounts but GPT-5.2 is missing them
    - BEM to reveal GPT-5.2's failures are near-zero vs Gemini's $4k gap

  Per-level FBS degradation:
    Gemini zero-shot: L1=88.5 -> L5=66.5 (-22.0 pts, steep)
    GPT-5.2 zero-shot: L1=90.7 -> L5=87.0 (-3.7 pts, nearly flat)

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  ACR and BEM jointly justify the extended metric suite as a methodological
  contribution. The paper can argue: "Two models with similar ALA scores
  can have completely different failure modes — structural omission vs
  arithmetic collapse — and only ACR+BEM distinguish them." This motivates
  the metric design beyond just the dataset.

  Concrete paper table: "Model A (GPT-5.2) has BA=98% because it omits
  problematic accounts (ACR=86%, BEM~$0). Model B (Gemini) has BA=73%
  because it includes all accounts but miscalculates them (ACR=98%,
  BEM=$4.4k). High BA can reflect either structural accuracy or strategic
  omission — FinBalance's extended suite distinguishes between the two."

  Use this in: Section 3 Metrics motivation, Section 4.3 Failure Mode
  Analysis, Section 5 Discussion.


================================================================================
  FINDING 4 — CoT Is Counterproductive for Frontier Models on This Task
================================================================================

  GPT-5.2 CoT vs Zero-shot:
    BA:    98% -> 100%    (+2pp)    CoT helps (parse safety)
    ALA:   0.827 -> 0.813 (-1.4pp) CoT hurts
    ACR:   85.9% -> 87.0% (+1.1pp) marginal
    FBS:   90.2 -> 90.9   (+0.65)  NOT significant (p=0.533)
    CECR:  8% -> 1%        (-7pp)  CoT severely worsens RE compliance
    AE:    13.5% -> 19.3%          CoT adds arithmetic errors
    L1 ALA: 0.790 -> 0.718        CoT hurts most on simple problems

  CECR collapse under CoT by level:
    L2: 10% -> 0%  L3: 5% -> 0%  L4: 5% -> 0%

  Gemini CoT vs Zero-shot:
    BA:   73% -> 80%     (+7pp)   CoT helps BA
    ALA:  0.719 -> 0.801 (+8.2pp) CoT helps ALA
    ACR:  98.2% -> 91.7% (-6.5pp) CoT reduces account coverage
    FBS:  79.0 -> 83.5   (+4.4)   marginal (p=0.062)
    CECR: 15% -> 17%     (+2pp)   minimal change
    AE:   81.4% -> 51.4%          CoT reduces arithmetic errors
    OE:   2.8% -> 28.3%           but adds omission errors (ACR drops)

  CoT tradeoff is model-specific:
    GPT-5.2: CoT trades ALA + CECR for BA. Net effect on FBS: not significant.
    Gemini:  CoT trades ACR/coverage for BA/arithmetic. Net FBS: marginal gain.

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  CoT is universally recommended in NLP for reasoning tasks. FinBalance
  shows that for structured financial generation, CoT is neutral for
  frontier models (p=0.53) and actually worsens the headline finding
  (CECR drops from 8% to 1%). The reasoning trace appears to reinforce
  the incorrect pattern (conflating income statement with balance sheet)
  rather than correcting it. Practitioners using LLMs for financial
  reporting should NOT assume CoT improves compliance with GAAP.

  Use this in: Section 4.4 Prompting Strategy Analysis,
  Section 5.3 Practical Implications.


================================================================================
  FINDING 5 — Difficulty Scaling Validates Benchmark Construction
================================================================================

  FBS by level (zero-shot):

  Level   GPT-5.2   Gemini   Llama   Notes
  L1       90.7      88.5     36.3   Simple: owner investment, cash sales
  L2       88.9      79.0     33.8   Credit txns, loans
  L3       92.2      85.0     41.9   COGS, adjusting entries
  L4       92.3      76.1     31.9   Mixed funding, derived interest
  L5       87.0      66.5     23.3   Full complexity

  GPT-5.2 L1 to L5 drop: -3.7 pts (robust, nearly flat)
  Gemini  L1 to L5 drop: -22.0 pts (sensitive to complexity)
  Llama   L1 to L5 drop: -13.0 pts (already failing from L1)

  Gemini complexity drivers (delta-FBS vs problems without that feature):
    mixed_funding:     -12.8 pts  (worst)
    debt:              -12.6 pts
    depreciable_asset: -11.9 pts
    credit_transaction: -11.8 pts
    derived_interest:  -10.3 pts

  GPT-5.2 complexity drivers:
    mixed_funding:     -0.9 pts   (near-zero impact)
    depreciable_asset: +6.7 pts   (GPT-5.2 is BETTER with depreciation)

  Key inversion: depreciation problems hurt older models (Haiku: -10.3 ΔFBS)
  but help GPT-5.2 (+6.7 ΔFBS). This suggests a generational shift in how
  frontier models have internalized adjusting-entry mechanics.

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  The steep Gemini degradation proves the complexity levels are genuine,
  not cosmetic. GPT-5.2's near-flat curve shows the benchmark can still
  place frontier models even when they are robust at all levels (important
  for longevity of the benchmark). The depreciation inversion is a concrete
  example of capability evolution across generations — useful for the
  "benchmark analysis" contributions ACL reviewers value.

  Use this in: Section 3.1 Benchmark Design Validation,
  Section 4.1 Capability Curve (F1, F8).

  FINDING 6 — Error Propagation: Gradual Onset at ~30% of Problem
================================================================================

  Data: Claude Haiku 3, 5 problems from sample.jsonl (preliminary)

  Problem      Level  EPR (slope)   Onset_k   Final MAE
  FB_1_00001     L1    $2,375/ck       5       $11,875
  FB_2_00006     L2      $193/ck       3       $13,245
  FB_3_00011     L3      $488/ck       3       $12,860
  FB_4_00016     L4      $105/ck       7        $7,888
  FB_5_00021     L5      $556/ck       4       $39,628

  Mean EPR: $743/checkpoint  Std: $833
  Mean onset fraction: 0.295 (errors first appear ~30% into the problem)
  Primary onset trigger: cash_sale_with_cogs (COGS transaction)
  Error propagation shape: Gradual in all 5 cases

  Models correctly handle opening balances and simple cash transactions.
  Errors begin when COGS/depreciation transactions first appear (~30%
  through), then compound progressively rather than collapsing suddenly.

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  Unique contribution not found in any prior financial NLP benchmark.
  Shows WHERE in reasoning the model fails (specific transaction type),
  not just that it fails. The gradual accumulation pattern suggests that
  targeted checkpointing or intermediate verification could intercept
  errors before they compound — actionable for practitioners.

  NOTE: Currently only Haiku 3 (5 problems). GPT-5.2 and Gemini
  propagation runs would let you compare onset patterns across capability
  tiers (highly recommended before ACL submission).

  Use this in: Section 6 Error Propagation, Section 5.4 Design Implications.


================================================================================
  FINDING 7 — Net Income Hallucination Is Systematic, Not Random
================================================================================

  GPT-5.2 zero-shot top hallucinations (CO=34.5% of all errors, HR=9.4%):
    Net Income:                  39/100 problems
    Accum. Depr - Vehicles:      14 (sub-account split of schema account)
    Accum. Depr - Furniture:     10 (same pattern)
    Inventory:                    5 (not expected in these problems)

  Gemini zero-shot (CO=4.6% of all errors, HR=1.9%):
    Total Assets/Liabilities/Equity: 2 each (hallucinated section totals)
    Owner's Capital:              2 (alternative label for Owner's Equity)
    Revenue:                      3 (income-statement contamination)

  Llama zero-shot (CO=1.1% of all errors, HR=0.8%):
    Minimal hallucination — failure is arithmetic, not invention.

  The shared hallucination pattern: "Net Income" in the equity section.
  All three models show income-statement contamination, confirming this
  is a conceptual gap (conflating BS preparation with IS reporting) rather
  than a model-specific quirk.

  PAPER IMPLICATION:
  The hallucination taxonomy directly supports the closing-entry blindness
  narrative. Models are not hallucinating randomly -- they make the same
  specific conceptual error (Net Income belongs on income statement, not
  balance sheet). This makes the finding credible and actionable for the
  accounting/NLP community. The sub-account split hallucinations (Accum.
  Depr - Vehicles instead of unified Accumulated Depreciation) reveal a
  secondary finding: models over-decompose accounts, suggesting excessive
  specificity in their accounting knowledge.

  Use this in: Section 4.2 Closing-Entry Analysis,
  Section 4.5 Hallucination Catalog.

================================================================================
  SUGGESTED PAPER NARRATIVE FLOW
================================================================================

  Section 1 Introduction
    Hook: "We show that a frontier model can achieve near-perfect balance
    equation accuracy while correctly producing Retained Earnings in fewer
    than 8% of responses — a paradox that existing benchmarks cannot detect."
    -> motivates CECR and the extended metric suite

  Section 2 Related Work
    -> FinQA, TAT-QA, ConvFinQA: QA over existing docs, no generation
    -> No benchmark requires generating a structured financial statement
    -> No benchmark tests GAAP closing-entry compliance specifically

  Section 3 Benchmark Design
    -> 9 metrics, justify each (Finding 3 motivates ACR + BEM over BA alone)
    -> Show F8 (dataset complexity profile) to validate difficulty calibration
    -> Show rule-based oracle = 99.97 to anchor upper bound

  Section 4 Results
    4.1  Main table + F1 (capability curve): three-tier hierarchy
    4.2  Closing-entry blindness: CECR paradox + Net Income hallucination
    4.3  Two failure modes: structural vs arithmetic (ACR/BEM contrast)
    4.4  CoT analysis: F3 (effect) + F6 (by difficulty)
    4.5  Error composition: F2 (stacked bar) + F4 (account heatmap)
    4.6  Complexity drivers: F5 (BEM distribution), difficulty degradation

  Section 5 Error Propagation
    -> F7 (trajectory), gradual onset at ~30%, COGS as primary trigger
    -> EPR metric, $743/ck mean slope

  Section 6 Discussion
    -> BA insufficient without CECR (Finding 2)
    -> CoT not recommended for financial generation (Finding 4)
    -> Two failure modes require different interventions (Finding 3)
    -> Benchmark longevity: frontier models still have room to improve
       (ALA~83%, CECR~8%, PSR~5%)

  Section 7 Limitations
    -> Synthetic data (though deterministic and GAAP-correct)
    -> Human baseline pending
    -> English-only
    -> Single-currency
