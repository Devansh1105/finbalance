\begin{abstract}
  Large language models are increasingly used in finance for document
  analysis, workflow automation, and decision support, but their
  reliability on core accounting tasks remains poorly understood.
  We introduce \benchmark{}, a dataset of 2{,}500 synthetic, realistic accounting
  problems for evaluating whether a model can construct a balance sheet
  from raw journal entries.
  Each instance is generated by a deterministic ledger engine and paired
  with exact ground truth, enabling precise measurement of equation
  satisfaction, account-level accuracy, account coverage, and
  closing-entry behavior.
  We report two evaluation tiers: a completed stratified 500-problem
  study spanning seven model families across zero-shot and
  chain-of-thought prompting, and a separate paired 100-problem
  compatibility study for additional frontier systems.
  On the completed 500-problem study, DeepSeek-v3.2 with \CoT{} reaches a
  FinBalance Score of \(92.25\) and \(99.4\%\) balance accuracy, while
  several zero-shot runs recover most expected accounts yet satisfy the
  accounting equation less than \(25\%\) of the time.
  The central finding is a capability split: models often recover the
  expected account set while still violating the accounting equation or
  mishandling retained-earnings closure.
  Fluent financial language therefore does not imply reliable
  bookkeeping, and exact accounting invariants expose failures that are
  easy to miss in answer-only financial benchmarks.
\end{abstract}
